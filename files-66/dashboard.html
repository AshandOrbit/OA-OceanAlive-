Salt Marsh & Ecosystem Building

Beyond Seagrass

Ocean Alive's work is expanding from seagrass meadows to an ecosystem-level approach – salt marshes, oyster reefs, and riparian zones. This is not scope creep. The Nature Restoration Regulation (Reg. 2024/1991) requires ecosystem-level targets, not species-level. The EU Carbon Removal Certification Framework (Reg. 2024/3012) recognises blue carbon across coastal wetlands, not just seagrass. And the Sado estuary's Annex I habitats include salt marsh types (1310, 1320, 1410, 1420) alongside seagrass (1110, 1140). Monitoring one without the other gives an incomplete picture of the system.

Why This Matters for the VCM Goal

If the VCM methodology only covers seagrass, the reference implementation is narrow. If it covers coastal wetlands – seagrass + salt marsh – the carbon stock is larger, the ecosystem service valuation is higher, and Ocean Alive's multi-habitat monitoring model becomes more valuable. Salt marshes store 2–5× more carbon per hectare than seagrass in some configurations. Adding them to the methodology multiplies the incentive for community-based MRV.

The System · River → Estuary → MPA → Offshore

The Sado isn't an isolated estuary. It's the hinge point in a connected system that runs from the headwaters in Serra da Vigia to the offshore Atlantic. What the Guardiãs monitor – seagrass health, water quality, sediment condition – is a downstream signal of upstream management and an upstream condition for offshore marine protection. The Guardiãs are monitoring the system's nervous system, whether or not they're framed that way.
